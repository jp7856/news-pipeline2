Improved and Optimized Prompt (Final Version)
[Workbook format: LEVEL 1 Multiple Choice Question Version]
I’ve uploaded an article in a document file. At the end of the article, there is a vocabulary list.
Make a four-part workbook based on the article and vocabulary list, following the detailed instructions below.

Style: concise, classroom-friendly, natural English
Rule: Avoid repeating the same target words across activities unless pedagogically necessary.

{ACTIVITY 1} – Vocabulary Synonyms
Source: Vocabulary list
Select four words from the vocabulary list. List the four words at the top. 
Of those four, use three to create short, clear fill-in-the-blank sentences that reflect the article’s usage. 
Make sure:
The context of each sentence reflects how the word is used in the article.
Sentences are concise (10–12 words), natural, and appropriate for A2 or A2-B1  learners.
Make new sentences that are not related to the content or topic of the article. Only the contextual meaning of the words is the same.
Make the length of the blanks 17 spaces, like the example below. 
Example (Format Only):
A. Fill in the blanks using the given words. (If necessary, change the form) 
costume   behavior  effect  symbol
1. Wearing a uniform can have a strong _________________ on how people act.
2. The child dressed up in a superhero _________________ for the Halloween party.
3. The teacher praised the students’ kind _________________ toward others.
✅ Tips:
Choose words that demonstrate both meaning and usage.
Keep one word per blank and ensure only one correct answer.
{ACTIVITY 2} – Unscramble the Sentences
Source: Article Text 
Select four sentences from the article. Split up each of the sentence into four parts. The divisions should have clear meaning (clause, subject/object, main information).  
Make sure:
The sentences are no longer than 20 words. If the sentences are longer, you may edit some of the details, but only as a last resort. 
Write the correct sentence (not divided) beneath the question. 
As much as possible avoid the vocabulary words used in Activity 1. But this can limit the choices and make the choice of sentences to be of less quality. If so, allow fo the overlap. 
Example (Format Only):
B. Unscramble and complete the sentences.  
1. was recently conducted / an interesting study / in Milan, Italy / in the metro system
2. stood / inside a subway car / wearing a Batman costume / a person
{ACTIVITY 3} – Comprehension Matching (Two-Choice Sentences)
Source: Article content (not vocabulary list)
Write three comprehension questions, each with four multiple choices. 
Requirements:
Each question should come from different parts of the article content.
Write the level of the question at a A2-B1 level. 
Write the answers below the questions.
Make the questions slightly challenging. Avoid questions that ask about simple information. 
Make one of the questions a true or false. (“What is true according to the article?” or “What is NOT true according to the article?”)
Example (Format Only):
C. Answer the question. 
Why did more passengers give up their seats in the study?
a) They were told to follow subway rules.
b) They became reminded of positive values.
c) They noticed the pregnant woman more clearly.
d) They felt pressured to do so by Batman. 
What measure did the officials take?
a) They fed the squirrel with nuts.
b) They warned people about a danger.
c) They called animal shelters for help.
d) They reported the missing pets with flyers.
{ACTIVITY 4} – Comprehension and Discussion
Source: Entire article.
Provide three comprehension questions and three discussion questions.
Make the questions at an A1-A2 level. 
The first comprehension question is copied from the article file, right underneath the headline. 
Write the remaining two comprehension questions. These questions should:  
Require complete-sentence answers.
Avoid yes/no or factual-only questions.
The first discussion question is copied from the article file, below the main text, right underneath the words “For Discussion” and above the vocabulary list. 
Write the remaining two comprehension questions. These questions should:
Be open-ended and conversational.
Encourage personal reflection or opinion.
Example (Format Only and only two examples):
Comprehension
1. Why did Dulcie Cowling give up her smartphone, and what changed for her?
2. How have some people replaced their smartphones after quitting them?
Discussion
1. Could you live without a smartphone? What would be hardest?
2. Do you think life is happier without smartphones? Why?
✅ Tips:
Keep all questions concise 
Make them sound natural and relevant to the article’s ideas.
✅ Checklist for AI
Four activities total (1–4), each with distinct focus.
Vocabulary and comprehension questions reflect article context.
No overlap between target words unless pedagogically justified.
Maintain Level 2 difficulty (B1–B2).
Language and tone: clear, concise, and suitable for classroom interaction.
{Extra Activity} phrases, clauses, or phrasal verbs
Note: Make this separately from the rest of the content. In other words overlaps do not need to be avoided.

● Write out the actual sentence from the article that is being used. Put the target phrase in bold.
● Write new sentences for the activity with a different topic instead of using the ones from the article. Write the sentences at the A2 level.
● Make three sets, one for each selected phrase or clause.

{ACTIVITY 1} – Choose Correct Term
Source: Article text.
Choose five words from the article, either from the vocabulary list or words that have meaning or educational value. Avoid proper names or details.  Write sample sentence that include the word. (written at the A2-B1 level.) Give a second word option so that the student has to choose the correct term. 
Make sure 
Make sure:
There is only one correct choice, and the context of each sentence reflects how the word is used in the article.
Sentences are concise (10–14 words), natural, and appropriate for A2–B1 learners.
Make new sentences that are not related to the content or topic of the article. Only the contextual meaning of the words is the same.
Example (Format Only):
A. Circle the right words. 
1. The students learned how the machine was (divided / decided) into multiple parts. 
2. The team hopes to win the match and (justify / qualify) for the final round.
3. The coach will (announce / pronounce) the team’s final roster tomorrow afternoon.
{ACTIVITY 2} – Find the Right Definition
Source: Vocabulary list.
Choose three vocabulary words or terms from the article. Write simple and brief definitions (at an A2 level) that are appropriate to context of the words or terms in the article. Randomize the order, so that the student has to match the correct word with its definition. 

Make sure:
Each left word/term clearly matches one right vocabulary only.
Use authentic expressions from the article.
Example (Format Only):
B. Find the right definition of each word. 
1. playoff 
2. powerhouse 
3. expectation 
a. a belief about what will probably happen
b. a team that is very strong and successful
c. an extra game to decide who advances to the next round

Source: Article content (not vocabulary list).

1. The 2026 World Cup will be hosted by three different nations.
2. Korea will play its first match in Washington, D.C.
3. Korea will compete against South Africa during the group stage.

{ACTIVITY 1} – Contextual Vocabulary Replacement (Double Blank)
Source: Article text
● Select a two-sentence passage from the article (about 30–45 words). They should be a passage from the article in the actual sequence, and not paraphrased.
Keep the overall wordcount of the passage low, optimally around 27 to 36 words by Microsoft Word standards. If no suitable two-sentence passage exists, use three sentences, but only as a last resort.
Remove a word from each of the two sentences (one blank per sentence; do not place two blanks in one sentence). 
Choose words that would have educational value. At least one removed word should come from the vocabulary list.
● Then, create four multiple-choice options for the blanks.
Two options should include one correct word but an incorrect second word. The correct word should be one of the words taken out from the sentence to make the blank.
One option should have both words incorrect.
The correct option should have synonyms (not the exact words) that fit both blanks accurately.
● Make two questions, even though the examples just have one. 
The activity would follow the formats below. 
Example (Format Only):
A. Which pair of words fits best in the blank?
The prime minister of Laos called the dam collapse the worst disaster ①__________ by the nation in decades. He ②_______________ that those responsible be identified and punished.
a) ① experienced – ② showed
b) ① revisited – ② insisted
c) ① faced – ② demanded
d) ① suffered – ② reached

a) ① was released – ② integrity

c) ① launched – ② media
d) ① debuted – ② engagement
✅ Tips:
Use contextually rich sentences (e.g., describing an event, process, or discovery).

{ACTIVITY 3} – Logical Sequencing (Three Sentences)
Source: Overall article content
● Write three concise sentences that summarize major points or events from the article.
The student’s task is to place them in the correct logical order.
● The goal of the activity is to check the student’s general understanding of the article content, but also the student’s ability to ascertain the logical flow and argument of the three sentences. 
● Make sure:
The sentences represent different aspects of the article. They can be paraphrased but should not be exactly the same as the article text. 
Only one possible correct order exists for the three sentences.
Sentences are short (10–18 words each) and suitable for classroom use. The sentences should ab at an A1-B2 level.
Example (Format Only):
C. Write the right order of the sentences.
a) The AI service, however, has resulted in lawsuits from publishers because it uses their content.
b) In fact, some companies claim that they are being forced to allow this if they wish to appear in Google’s search results.
c) A popular feature on Google allows people to get quick summaries of information.
C. Write the right order of the sentences.
a) The winners of the Ig Nobel Prize looked into painted cows.
b) The scientists wanted to see if the striped cows would discourage insects, much like zebras.
c) The animals were given zebra-style patterns on their bodies.
✅ Tips:
Focus on the article’s main logical or chronological flow.
Avoid focus on trivial details or minor examples.
Make each sentence independently clear so that order is determined by reasoning, not guesswork.

{ACTIVITY 3} – Summary Matching (Fill-in and Sequence)
