Rome has turned part of its subway into a living museum by displaying ancient Roman relics inside new stations. The project, linked to the opening of Line C near the Colosseum, allows daily commuters to see history during their regular journeys.
City officials decided to place the finds in stations because major ruins were uncovered during construction. Instead of hiding them in storage, Rome chose glass cases, information panels, and carefully designed spaces to protect the objects and make archaeology part of everyday life.
The displays include pottery, stone wells, sections of ancient walls, and remains of Roman baths. Some areas show mosaics and large structures exactly where they were found underground, giving visitors a strong sense of the city’s past.

Denmark Ends Letter Delivery After 400 Years
Denmark has made a historic decision: it will stop delivering letters through its national postal service. PostNord, the company responsible for mail in Denmark, announced that the final letter will be sent on December 30, 2025. This marks the end of a tradition that began in 1624, when King Christian IV introduced one of Europe’s earliest postal systems. While letters will disappear, PostNord will continue to deliver parcels, which have become far more important in the age of online shopping. Many of Denmark’s famous red mailboxes have already been removed, and some were even sold to the public as collectibles.
The main reason for this change is the dramatic fall in letter use. Over the past 25 years, the number of letters sent in Denmark has dropped by 90 percent. Today, most communication takes place online, through email, text messages, and digital platforms. Denmark is considered one of the most digitalized countries in the world, and this shift has made traditional letters almost unnecessary. At the same time, PostNord has faced serious financial problems. In 2024, the company reported a deficit of 428 million Danish krone (around €57 million). Maintaining letter delivery was simply too costly.
Reactions among Danish citizens have been mixed. Some feel nostalgic, saying that letters and red mailboxes were part of the nation’s cultural identity. Others argue that the decision is practical, since letters are rarely used and packages are more relevant to modern life.
Denmark is not alone in facing this challenge. In the United Kingdom, regular mail is now delivered only two or three days a week, while France has raised stamp prices. These examples suggest that Denmark’s move may be part of a wider global trend toward digital communication.
