Denmark Says Goodbye to Traditional Mail
Denmark’s state-owned postal service, PostNord, has officially delivered its final letters, marking the end of a 400-year-old national tradition. Effective December 30th, the company ceased its "universal service obligation" for physical mail to focus exclusively on parcel delivery. 
This transition involves the removal of approximately 1,500 iconic red mailboxes from streets across the country and the layoff of roughly 1,500 employees. While private operators like Dao will continue to handle letters, citizens must now visit specific shops to post mail or pay extra for home collection, as the state no longer guarantees cheap, doorstep service.
The primary driver for this radical shift is the rapid digitalization of Danish society. Denmark is one of the most digital nations in the world, utilizing systems like MitID for almost all official communication. Consequently, letter volumes have plummeted by over 90 percent since 2000, dropping from 1.4 billion items to just 110 million. 
Today, the average Dane receives only one physical letter per month. Maintaining a nationwide network for such low volume became financially unsustainable, especially as the price of a single stamp skyrocketed to over 4 dollars to cover rising costs.
Public reaction to the move has been a mix of pragmatism and concern. Many younger Danes, who rarely use physical mail, view the change as a natural progression. However, advocacy groups and unions have criticized the decision, citing a "crisis" for the roughly 271,000 citizens—mostly elderly or low-income—who are exempt from digital mail and still rely on physical letters for medical or legal documents. Critics argue that moving to a private monopoly like Dao could lead to higher prices and less reliable service for these vulnerable populations.
Denmark’s decision makes it a "canary in the coal mine" for other nations facing similar digital shifts. Norway is already considering government reports that recommend ending regular postbox deliveries in favor of centralized pickup points. Meanwhile, postal services in Canada and the United Kingdom are watching closely as they struggle with their own declining mail volumes, suggesting that the era of the traditional postman may soon come to a close across much of the developed world
